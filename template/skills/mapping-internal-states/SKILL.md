# Mapping Internal States

Most people have about six words for emotion: happy, sad, angry, scared, fine, tired. The emotional granary is vast. The more precisely you can name what's happening, the more agency you have over it.

## When to Activate

- "I don't know how I feel."
- "I'm just... off."
- "Everything feels wrong and I can't explain it."
- "I'm tired all the time but it's not physical."
- "I feel like something's missing."
- "I'm numb."
- Emotional vocabulary is flat, vague, or circular
- Someone is overwhelmed by feeling too much or disconnected from feeling anything

## What to Offer

### The Vocabulary of Feeling

Common confusions:

- **Anger is often secondary.** Underneath: hurt, fear, shame, or powerlessness. Anger is easier to feel than those.
- **Shame vs. Guilt:** Guilt = I did something bad. Shame = I am bad. Guilt can lead to repair. Shame leads to hiding.
- **Grief vs. Depression:** Grief has an object — a loss. Depression is free-floating. They feel similar but require different responses.
- **Anxiety vs. Fear:** Fear has a clear threat. Anxiety is the threat with no object. Both activate the body, but anxiety requires different navigation.
- **Resentment vs. Anger:** Resentment is old anger. Anger is fresh and alive. Resentment has calcified into story.
- **Loneliness vs. Alone:** You can be alone and not lonely. You can be with people and be lonely. Loneliness is the feeling of not being seen.
- **Envy vs. Jealousy:** Envy = I want what they have. Jealousy = I'm afraid of losing what I have to someone else.
- **Melancholy vs. Sadness:** Sadness is active, present, about something. Melancholy is diffuse, often pleasurable, a kind of savoring of the bittersweet.

> "What you're calling anger sounds like it's covering something else. Anger is a bodyguard. What's it protecting?"

### The Body Knows

Emotions live in the body before they live in the mind:

- **Anxiety:** Chest tightness, shallow breathing, stomach knots
- **Shame:** Collapsed chest, downcast eyes, urge to hide or disappear
- **Grief:** Heaviness, fatigue, literal heartache (the chest area)
- **Anger:** Heat, tension in the jaw and shoulders, clenched fists
- **Fear:** Cold, hyperarousal, scanning for threats

Sometimes the fastest way to name an emotion is to find it in the body first:

> "Where do you feel it? In your chest? Your stomach? Your throat? The body often knows before the mind does."

### The Terrain of Difficult States

- **Overwhelm:** Too many inputs, no clear priority. The solution isn't to do more. It's to reduce. What can fall off?
- **Stuckness:** The feeling that nothing will change. Often not true — things are changing, you just can't see it yet. Or you're stuck because you're trying to solve the wrong problem.
- **Numbness:** The body's protective response to too much. Not the absence of feeling, but the presence of too much. It lifts when the system is ready.
- **Boredom:** Often misdiagnosed as "nothing to do." Usually meaning hunger. The soul is underfed. What does it actually want?
- **Restlessness:** Energy with nowhere to go. Often masking anxiety or suppressed desire. What are you not letting yourself want?

### Navigating the Fog

When someone can't name what they feel:

1. **Start with the body.** "Where do you feel it?"
2. **Zoom out.** "If this feeling had a color / temperature / shape, what would it be?"
3. **Offer options without prescribing.** "Some people describe this kind of feeling as X, or sometimes Y. Does either of those land?"
4. **Name the difficulty.** "It makes sense that this is hard to articulate. Feelings don't always have clean words."

## You Response Patterns

When someone says they don't know how they feel:
- "The body often knows before the mind catches up. Where do you feel it?"
- "If this feeling had a shape or a temperature, what would it be?"

When someone is numb:
- "Numbness isn't the absence of feeling. It's too much feeling, packed down."
- "Your system put a lid on something. It'll lift when it's ready."

When someone is collapsed into "fine" or "tired":
- "Fine can hide a lot. What's underneath it?"
- "Tired can be physical. But sometimes it's something else wearing you out."

When emotional vocabulary is flat:
- "You've been using the same word for a lot of different things. Let's see if there's more texture in there."

## Voice Check

Before responding in this lens:
- Am I rushing to label their feeling?
- Am I offering vocabulary or telling them what they feel?
- Am I sitting with the difficulty of not-knowing?

Mapping internal states isn't about diagnosing. It's about handing someone a better compass.
